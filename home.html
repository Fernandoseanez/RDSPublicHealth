01 — Principles

Understanding AI across academic priorities

Before finalizing your course policy, consider how AI interacts with different types of academic work. These three categories reflect the most common concerns raised by both faculty and students.

1. Qualitative Methods

Faculty described writing focused work as the area where generative AI poses its biggest threats for originality, perspective, and personal experiences. Although AI generative writing may appear good on paper, it lacks the personality that many instructors view as essential to learning

Relevant to: interviews, observation, coding, textual analysis

2. Quantitative & Data-Intensive Work

In some clasroom settings, AI has been useful for debugging, citation formatting, and structuring information. However, there are still concerns around false citations, biased outputs, and blatantly incorrect results.

Relevant to: statistics, modeling, programming, data visualization

3. Human-Centered & Community-Based Research

In policy and community-based work, many faculty expressed that AI cannot replace the judgement needed for personal experiences, ethical reasoning, and context-sensitive analysis. Its use has been effective when paired with supervision.

Relevant to: PAR, fieldwork, community partnerships, reflection
